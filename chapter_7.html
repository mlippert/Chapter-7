Chapter 7: Stereoisomerism

Looking back:

To begin this chapter you should be familiar with: bonding, molecular geometry, structure, and conformations of molecules.

To be discovered:

In this chapter, you will explore the symmetry and asymmetry of molecules. The arrangement of atoms in space for a molecule has consequences for its identity, physical properties, and reactivity. Two compounds displaying identical atomic connectivity, but with a different three-dimensional atomic arrangement, may exhibit significantly dissimilar behavior.

Looking forward:

Stereoisomerism permeates nearly all aspects of organic chemistry. It is an inseparable part of many chemical reactions. Recognition of when and how it is applied is a required skill. Every chapter that follows will have some degree of dependency on stereoisomerism.

Connections to Nature:

The prevalence of right-handedness has had important consequences in our society. Approximately ninety percent of humans are right-handed. This preference was an important consideration in early architecture and construction design.

Of particular note are the spiral staircases in many medieval castles. They share a fascinating structural feature in that they generally ascend in a clockwise pattern. This improved the defensibility of the castle. An invading combatant (most commonly right-handed) would find that the central pillar of the staircase restricts their attack.

Conversely, the defender would have greater space to maneuver their weapon. A subset of some armies was trained to fight exclusively with their left hands to counter this defensive feature and improve their tactical chances.

Connections to the Health Professions:

Place your hand over your heart. The odds are excellent that you took your right hand and placed it on the left side of your chest. A person with the congenital condition of situs inversus with dextrocardia, however, would need to do the opposite to accomplish this.

Situs inversus (Latin, inverted position) affects less than 0.01% of the population. This condition takes two forms. In situs inversus with levocardia (Latin., levo-, left) the heart remains in its normal position , but the other organs are switched. In situs inversus with dextrocardia (Latin, dextro-, right) all of the internal organs are switched including the heart.

Computed tomography (CT) scanning and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) are commonly used to identify the condition. Diagnosing this condition before surgery would be necessary. Individuals who need an organ transplant require special consideration. The heart has a “handedness” to it. With dextrocardia the heart not only resides on the left, but it is the mirror image of a normal heart. Receiving a donor heart from a person not affected by the condition requires careful reorientation of the blood vessels.
