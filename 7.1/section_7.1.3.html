7.1.3 Stereoisomerism in Allenes

Allenes can also be stereogenic. Keep in mind that the two substituents on the ends of an allene are perpendicular to the two on the opposite end. The bonding in an allene was previously discussed [LINK to allene discussion in bonding chapter]. Like alkenes, the allene has two stereocenters, but it constitutes a single stereogenic unit in that only two stereoisomers are possible. Switching both groups at each end of the allene results in the original structure being regenerated.

TIP: Donâ€™t forget that the central carbon of an allene is often represented as a dot, separating the two alkene units.

For an allene to be stereogenic, the two substituents attached to a common end carbon must be different. The identity of the substituents on opposite ends of the structure is irrelevant.

[INTERACTIVE: Animate (touch-activate) the switching of the two groups and then show how the two structures cannot be superposed on top of each other. Allow students to select the carbon that will have the two groups switched, and then they can then drag the new structure on top of the original structure to verify that they are not superposable. Rotation and translation will be important manipulations. It might also be useful for the allene case to allow students to switch BOTH stereocenters and then confirm that the original structure is regenerated.]
