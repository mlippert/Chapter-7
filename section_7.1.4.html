7.1.4 Stereoisomerism in Biphenyl and Related Derivatives

Stereoisomerism can also result from conformational restrictions. Examples of this are seen in ortho-substituted biphenyl compounds. These compounds might appear to participate in conjugation across both rings, but nonbonded interactions between the ortho substituents can create torsional strain forcing the rings out of coplanarity. Biphenyl itself (hydrogens at the ortho positions) adopts a conformation that places the panels of the aromatic rings at a 40˚ dihedral angle lessening the ability of the π systems to interact. With increasingly larger substituents, relief of steric interactions more than compensates for the loss of resonance between the rings.

[TOGGLE: Let student toggle on/off the blue Hs (and their bonds).]

If the two ortho substituents that confront each other are small such as -H, -F, -Cl, -CH3, and -OCH3 they can rotate past one another with relative ease. With larger, sterically more demanding substituents such as -SO3H, -Br, -I, and -t-Bu the barrier to rotation becomes unattainable. It is in these latter cases that stereoisomers can result provided that the two ortho substituents on each aromatic ring are different.

Stereoisomers whose existence arises from restricted rotation about a single bond are atropisomers. This new term is introduced since these stereoisomers can result from rotation about a single bond, rather than switching two substituents. Despite the interconversion between atropisomers occurring via a forcible conformational change it is still instructive to approach the analysis of the structure in terms of stereocenters. Stereogenic biphenyl compounds contain two stereocenters, specifically, the two carbons connecting each aromatic ring. Switching the two groups attached to a biphenyl stereocenter requires you to imagine overturning the entire aromatic ring. Analogous to stereogenic alkenes and allenes, it is acceptable to consider the biphenyl group as a whole a stereogenic unit.
