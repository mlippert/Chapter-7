7.1.2 Stereoisomerism at Tetrahedral Carbon

A carbon with tetrahedral geometry is a stereocenter if switching two of the groups attached to it results in a stereoisomer. We already have some experience in this area from our earlier discussion of disubstituted cycloalkanes [Link to Conformational Analysis of Disubstituted Cycloalkanes].

The descriptors cis and trans were used earlier to make the distinction between what we now know to be a pair of stereoisomers. Inspection of cis-1,4-dimethyl cyclohexane reveals that by switching one of the methyl groups with a hydrogen on the same carbon results in the trans isomer. These two structures are non-superposable and are, therefore, stereoisomers.

It can be concluded that both of the substituted carbons in 1,4-dimethylcyclohexane are stereocenters. Switching both stereocenters returns the original compound.

A similar observation of stereoisomerism is made for 2-bromobutane. The bromine is attached to a tetrahedral carbon, and we should consider the possibility of drawing it projecting forward and receding back.

It is necessary to test how these versions relate by attempting to superpose them. Simple translocation (sliding in the plane of the display) of one structure onto the other shows that the methyl and ethyl group align exactly, but the bromine and hydrogen do not coincide. There is no manipulation possible that will permit these two structures to fit precisely into one another. Again, note how both structures have identical connectivity, but their spatial arrangement is dissimilar. They too are stereoisomers.

A tetrahedral carbon in possession of four different groups is referred to as an asymmetric carbon and will always be a stereocenter. It is a common feature in many organic structures. It is a specific type of stereocenter and will be given much attention in upcoming sections.

TIP: It important to remember that wedge and hash notation may be drawn from an atom whether or not it is stereogenic. There are times when emphasizing the tetrahedral nature of carbon might be important whether or not it is asymmetric. Always check carefully that the atom under inspection has four different groups attached.

Bromochlorofluoromethane possesses an asymmetric carbon. Switching any two of the groups attached to carbon produces a compound that is not superposable on the original structure.
